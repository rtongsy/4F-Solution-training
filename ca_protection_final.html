4F Solution — Canadian Track: Protection

Building the right protection plan — for this client, not the average one

Discovery told you what the client has, what they owe, who depends on them, and what matters most. This module teaches what to do with that — how to identify the right products, cover the need completely, sequence priorities when budget is tight, manage cost intelligently, and connect every recommendation to values.

How to read the discovery data and identify what is actually at risk. The decision logic for matching each need to the right product. 4F Solution — Canadian Lesson 1: Reading the need

Before any product — what is actually at risk?

A product recommendation without a defined need is a guess dressed as advice. Four questions must be answered before any product conversation begins.

Question 1 — Income
What income would stop — and for how long does the plan need it replaced?
A 35-year-old with a 25-year mortgage and two young children needs income replaced for a fundamentally different period than a 55-year-old whose mortgage is paid. The duration shapes the product.
Question 2 — Obligations
What survives regardless of what happens to the client?
The mortgage still comes due. Children still need to be fed and educated. Debt still accrues. List every obligation the plan depends on the client funding — and what happens to each one if they cannot. That list is the gap the protection plan exists to close.
Question 3 — Existing coverage
What does it actually provide — not what does it say on paper?
Group life at 2x salary sounds like coverage until you put it next to a $700,000 mortgage. Group LTD at 70% sounds adequate until you calculate the after-tax benefit when the employer pays the premium. The professional reads the actual policy — not the summary card.
Question 4 — The gap
Expressed in specific dollars, for each risk event.
Not "they need more coverage." The gap is: if this client dies tomorrow, their family faces a $640,000 shortfall. If they become disabled, their monthly income drops by $1,800 against obligations of $4,200. Each number connects to a specific product conversation. Vague gaps produce vague recommendations.
The discipline
Do not open a product conversation until all four questions have answers. A professional who can say "here is exactly what you are exposed to, and here is exactly what closes each gap" is operating at a different level than one who presents a menu of products and monthly costs.

When the sequence shifts — read the profile
Single, no dependants → disability and CI before life. Family history of cancer → weight CI higher. Physically demanding occupation → disability probability rises. Young family + large mortgage → term life may move alongside disability given its low cost. This is a framework, not a formula.
Name what is left open
"Here is what we are closing today. Here is what remains exposed. Here is when we address it." A client who understands their own exposure and has a plan is far better served than one who received a partial plan without knowing what was missing.
4F Solution — Canadian Lesson 6: Managing cost

Not the cheapest plan — the most efficient one

Maximum coverage per dollar of available cash flow. Every dollar of unnecessary premium is a dollar that could close another gap.

Tool 1
Match term to obligation
A 30-year term is only justified if the obligation runs 30 years. Match the term length to the actual horizon — mortgage payoff, child independence, retirement.
Tool 2
Elimination period = emergency reserve
Client has 90 days of reserves → 90-day elimination period. Lower premium, same protection. The emergency fund and disability policy are designed together.
Tool 3
Layer group + individual
Individual coverage fills the gap — does not replace group. Size individual to the difference between after-tax group benefit and actual obligations.
Tool 4
Riders: value vs habit
✓ Own occupation · COLA on disability · Waiver of premium · Convertibility on term
✗ Accidental death · ROP on CI unless all other gaps funded
Combining policies
Tool 5 — Term laddering
Match multiple smaller policies to the declining obligation curve. Example: $500K mortgage + $300K income replacement need.

Covers mortgage + dependant years
$300K · 10-year term

Always confirm survivor provisions exist in the specific policy. Always compare actual quotes.
Tool 7 — Joint last-to-die
Estate planning tool only. Pays on second death. Funds tax liability, estate equalization, or legacy. Not for income replacement or debt coverage.
Tool 8 — Multi-life policy
Two individual policies under one number. Each partner keeps their own coverage, convertibility rights, and benefit period. Saves $30–$90/year vs two separate policies. No structural drawbacks.
The discipline
Every structural decision should be driven by what it achieves for the client — not by what reduces premium most aggressively. Joint first-to-die is not simply cheaper or simpler than two individual policies. It is a different structure with specific provisions, specific limitations, and specific situations where it fits. Understand it fully before recommending it.
4F Solution — Canadian Lesson 7: Values connection

Connecting the plan to what the client said matters most

A technically correct recommendation that is emotionally disconnected will not be acted on. The client who understands what they are buying in terms of what they value — not what it costs — is the client who says yes, maintains the plan, and refers others.

The language shift that changes everything
Not: "You need $750,000 of life insurance and the premium is $87 per month." Instead: "You told me that your family's security is what matters most — that no matter what happens to you, your partner and your children are taken care of. Here is exactly what happens to that security if you die tomorrow with what you have now. And here is exactly what changes if we close this gap. The cost is $87 per month. The question is whether your family's security is worth $87 per month to you." The numbers are the same. The framing is entirely different.
Step 1 — Recall the value, not the product
Begin every recommendation by reflecting what the client told you during discovery.
Not "you said security was important" — but "you told me that if something happened to you, the one thing you could not accept was your children losing the house and your partner having to rebuild from scratch." That specificity tells the client you were listening. It anchors every recommendation to something they already believe — not something you are trying to convince them of.
Step 2 — Show the consequence of the gap
Make the exposure real — in their numbers, against their stated priority.
"If you died tomorrow, your partner would receive $160,000 from your group plan. The mortgage is $480,000. After paying it off, there is $320,000 left — roughly four years of living expenses for your family. After that, your partner is on their own. Is that the security you described?" The client answers that question. The professional does not editorialize.
Step 3 — Present the solution as the gap closed
The product is what closes the gap — not what the professional is selling.
"This policy ensures that if you die tomorrow, your partner has enough to pay off the mortgage, maintain the family's lifestyle for ten years, and fund your children's education. What you described as security — this is what funds it." The premium follows naturally. It is not the lead — it is the cost of the outcome the client said they want.
Step 4 — Handle cost objections with values, not discounts
Most cost objections are uncertainty objections in disguise.
When a client says "that seems expensive," the professional who responds by reducing the coverage has missed what the client is actually expressing. They are not sure the risk is real, or not sure the product will deliver. Return to the gap: "Let me show you what happens if we don't close this." If after seeing the consequence clearly they still decline, they are making an informed decision about an accepted risk. That is their right. Document it and move forward — never reduce coverage to overcome an objection that was never actually about the premium.
The plan the client owns
A client who understands their protection plan in terms of their own values — not in premiums and policy numbers — is a client who maintains that plan through rate changes, life transitions, and competing financial pressures. They know what it is protecting. They know why it matters. That is the difference between a transaction and a relationship.
