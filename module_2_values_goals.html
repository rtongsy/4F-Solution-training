4F Solution — Module 2

Values and goals discovery

Before any recommendation is made, the professional must understand what the client is actually trying to protect, build, and leave behind. This module teaches you how to uncover that — in their words, not yours.

7 lessons
5 knowledge checks
What you will learn
Why values must come before any product conversation. How to ask discovery questions word for word. The most common client values and what they mean for the plan. How to identify short, medium, and long-term goals. How to connect what you hear directly to the 4 Fundamentals.
4F Solution — Lesson 1: Why values come first

Products answer the wrong question if you ask too soon

Every product in financial services exists to solve a problem. The mistake most professionals make is identifying the product before they understand the problem.

What happens when you lead with products
The client evaluates the product — not their situation. They compare price, not value. They feel sold to, not advised. And you miss the real issue because you never asked about it.
What happens when you lead with values
The client tells you what matters most to them. Every recommendation that follows is connected to something they already care about. The conversation feels like planning — because it is.
The rule that changes everything
Never mention a product until you can connect it directly to something the client told you matters to them. If you cannot make that connection, you are not ready to recommend it.
4F Solution — Lesson 2: Values vs goals

Values are the why. Goals are the what.

These two things are often confused in discovery conversations. Understanding the difference makes your questions sharper and your recommendations more defensible.

Values — the why
Values are what the client believes in and wants to protect. They are emotional and enduring. They do not change with market conditions or life circumstances. Examples: family security, independence, legacy, freedom from debt, peace of mind.
Goals — the what
Goals are the specific outcomes the client wants to achieve. They are measurable and time-bound. They flow from values. Examples: retire at 60, pay off the mortgage in 10 years, save $500,000, fund the children's education.
Why the order matters
Values come first because they tell you why a goal matters. A client who wants to retire at 60 because they value time with family will make different trade-offs than one who values status or financial independence. Same goal — completely different plan.
How they connect to the 4 Fundamentals
Values tell you which Fundamentals matter most to this client and in what order. A client who values family security above all will weight Protection heavily. One who values legacy will drive toward Tax Efficiency at transfer. The 4F Solution applies to everyone — values determine the emphasis.
4F Solution — Lesson 3: Common client values

What clients value most — and what it means for the plan

Most clients express one or two dominant values that shape every financial decision they make. Tap each value to see what it means for the 4F conversation.

Cash Flow tells you how long they survive without income. Protection closes the gap if something goes wrong. Growth builds the buffer. Tax Efficiency ensures nothing erodes it unnecessarily. The discovery question: "If your income stopped tomorrow, how long could your family maintain their lifestyle?"
Protection is paramount — their family's lifestyle depends on them. Cash Flow determines what they can sustain today. Growth builds what the family inherits. Tax Efficiency ensures transfers happen cleanly. The discovery question: "If something happened to you unexpectedly, what would you want to make sure was taken care of?"
Cash Flow is the starting point — freedom requires surplus. Growth builds the asset base that creates independence. Protection ensures one event does not eliminate what they have built. Tax Efficiency keeps more of it working for them. The discovery question: "What does financial independence look like to you — and when do you want to achieve it?"
Protection is critical — estate erosion is the biggest threat to what they leave behind. Tax Efficiency determines how much actually transfers. Growth and Cash Flow fund the life they live on the way there. The discovery question: "When you think about what you want to leave behind — whether to family, a cause, or a community — what does that look like?"
Cash Flow is the engine — eliminating debt requires consistent surplus. Growth accelerates the timeline. Protection ensures a setback does not reset the progress. Tax Efficiency keeps more available to accelerate payoff. The discovery question: "What would it mean to you to have no debt — and what would you do differently if you got there?"
All four Fundamentals contribute — but Protection and having a documented plan are often the most powerful for this client. They want to know it is all organized. The discovery question: "Is there anything about your financial situation right now that keeps you up at night — or that you know you should address but haven't?"
4F Solution — Lesson 4: Booking the appointment

Getting in front of the client — word for word

The values conversation only happens if you get the meeting. How you position the outreach determines whether the client shows up curious or defensive.

The single most important shift
Never position the meeting as a financial review, a product presentation, or a check-in. Position it as a conversation about what matters to them — because that is exactly what it is. Clients say yes to conversations about their life. They hesitate about meetings that sound like sales calls.
How to position the meeting
The meeting is not about products, accounts, or numbers. It is about understanding what the client is trying to protect, build, and leave behind — and whether their current situation supports that. Frame it that way every time.
Word for word — what to say
Phone outreach
"Hi [name], I work with a lot of people at your stage of life and I find most of them have never had a conversation that looks at their full financial picture at once — not just one product or account. I would love to set aside 30 minutes to have that conversation with you. No agenda other than understanding what matters most to you. Would [day] or [day] work?"
Email or message outreach
"Hi [name] — I have been thinking about reaching out because I work with a lot of people who are at a similar point in life and I find most have never had someone sit down with them and look at the full picture. I would love to have a 30-minute conversation — just to understand what is important to you and whether there are any gaps worth looking at. No pressure, no products. Would you be open to that?"
Referral outreach
"Hi [name] — [referrer's name] suggested I reach out. They thought you might find it valuable to have a conversation about your financial picture — not a sales call, just a conversation about where you are and where you want to go. I do this kind of discovery conversation with all my clients before anything else. Would you be open to 30 minutes?"
Handling objections
"I already have someone who handles that"
"That is great — I am not here to replace anyone. I just find that most people, even those with professionals already in place, have never had someone look at all four areas of their financial life in a single conversation. It only takes 30 minutes and you may find it useful regardless of what you decide to do after."
"I am not really looking for anything right now"
"That is completely fine — this is not about selling you anything. It is just a conversation about where you are financially and what matters most to you. A lot of people find it valuable even when they are not actively looking. Would you be open to 30 minutes just to see if anything is worth looking at?"
"Can you just send me some information first?"
"I would rather not — because the conversation is really about you, and I would not know what to send until I understand what matters most to you. That is the whole point of the 30 minutes. It is genuinely just a conversation, not a presentation."
The goal of the booking conversation
Get the 30 minutes. Nothing more. The values conversation happens in the meeting — not on the phone and not over email. Your only job in the outreach is to lower the barrier enough that they say yes.
4F Solution — Lesson 5: Discovery questions

The questions to ask — word for word

These questions are designed to open the conversation, not close a sale. Use them in sequence. Listen more than you talk. Let the client's answers guide where you go next.

Opening question
"Before we talk about anything specific — tell me, what does financial security mean to you and your family?"
Why it works: It is open, non-threatening, and immediately positions the conversation as being about them — not about products. Whatever they say next tells you what matters most.
Values question
"When you think about your financial life five years from now — what does success look like?"
Why it works: Moves the client from the present to a future they care about. Surfaces values through the language they use to describe that future.
Fear question
"What is the one financial risk that concerns you most — the thing you most want to make sure never happens?"
Why it works: Clients often know their fears better than their goals. This question surfaces the Protection conversation naturally, without you introducing it.
Legacy question
"If you could make sure one thing was taken care of for the people you love — what would that be?"
Why it works: Brings in legacy and estate thinking without using financial jargon. Clients answer this emotionally — which is exactly where the real plan lives.
Priority question
"If you could only fix one thing about your financial situation this year — what would it be?"
Why it works: Forces prioritization. The answer tells you where to start and which Fundamental to address first for this specific client.
4F Solution — Lesson 6: Goal-setting

Short, medium, and long-term goals — and why all three matter

Goals without a timeline are wishes. Once you understand a client's values, your job is to translate them into specific, time-bound goals across three horizons.

Short term
0–2 years
Emergency fund. Debt reduction. Coverage gaps. Getting organized.
Medium term
2–10 years
Home purchase. Education savings. Business goals. Income growth targets.
Long term
10+ years
Retirement. Legacy planning. Financial independence. Estate transfer.
The goal-setting question
"Let's think about three timeframes. In the next year or two, what is the most important thing to get right financially? In the next five to ten years, what are you working toward? And longer term — what does the end of your working life look like, and what do you want it to feel like?"
How goals connect to the 4 Fundamentals
Short-term goals often reveal Cash Flow and Protection gaps. Medium-term goals drive Growth conversations. Long-term goals bring in Tax Efficiency — how money is structured today determines how much is available at retirement and transfer. Every goal the client names maps to at least one Fundamental.
4F Solution — Lesson 7: Connecting to the 4 Fundamentals

What you heard — and where it goes in the plan

Once the discovery conversation is complete, the professional's job is to map what the client said to the 4 Fundamentals. This is what turns a conversation into a plan.

Cash flow
What they said about income, expenses, debt, and how much is left at the end of the month. Any goal that depends on surplus — which is all of them — starts here.
Protection
What they said about fears, risks, and what they cannot afford to have happen. Every gap between where they are and where they want to be is a protection conversation waiting to happen.
Growth
What they said about goals, timelines, and what they are building toward. Medium and long-term goals almost always require a growth strategy — registered accounts, investments, or both.
Tax efficiency
What they said about what they want to keep, transfer, or leave behind. Tax efficiency is the difference between what they earn and what they actually get to use — at every stage of life.
The bridging statement
"Based on what you have shared with me, I want to look at four areas together — because all of them connect directly to what you told me matters most. Let me show you what I mean."
