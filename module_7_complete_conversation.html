4F Solution — Module 7

The complete 4F Solution

Discovery is only half of the process. This module teaches the other half — how to take everything learned across the four Fundamentals, build a complete values-driven plan, and present it so every recommendation connects directly to something the client said matters to them.

8 lessons
5 knowledge checks
What you will learn
How the four discovery conversations connect into a single picture. The principles for building a complete plan from discovery notes. How to structure the solution delivery meeting. How to connect every recommendation to a stated value or goal. How to handle the most common objections. The one-page client summary — what it includes and how to use it. What happens after the plan is accepted.
4F Solution — Lesson 1: How the 4F process connects

The discovery is one conversation — not four separate ones

Each Fundamental builds on the last. By the time the professional reaches Tax Efficiency, they have a complete picture of the client's financial life — and every recommendation that follows is connected to something real.

V
Values — the foundation of everything
What the client cares about most. Every recommendation in the complete plan traces back to a value the client expressed here. This is why values come first — they are the lens through which the entire plan is evaluated.
1
Cash Flow — the engine
What comes in, what goes out, what remains. Surplus is the number everything else is built around. Without it, no other Fundamental can be properly funded.
2
Protection — the foundation
The four risks that can erase the plan. Every gap identified here becomes a recommendation in the complete plan — connected to the specific value it threatens.
3
Growth — the direction
Where surplus goes, across which vehicles, aligned to which time horizons and goals. Every growth recommendation is sized by what cash flow makes available and protected by what protection ensures.
4
Tax Efficiency — the multiplier
How the client keeps as much as possible at accumulation, distribution, and transfer. Tax efficiency is the final layer — it does not replace the other Fundamentals, it amplifies them.
What the professional has at the end of discovery
A complete picture of the client's values, their financial engine, the risks that threaten it, the goals they are building toward, and the tax position that determines how much they keep. That is everything needed to build a plan that is specific, defensible, and connected to what the client actually cares about.
4F Solution — Lesson 2: Building the complete plan

Every recommendation must connect to a value or goal the client expressed

The complete plan is not a product list. It is a structured response to everything the client told you — organized by the four Fundamentals, sized by surplus, and anchored to the values that opened the conversation.

The rule that governs every recommendation
Before including any recommendation in the plan, the professional must be able to complete this sentence: "I am recommending this because you told me that _____ matters to you, and without it, _____." If that sentence cannot be completed, the recommendation does not belong in the plan.
Step 1 — Start with the values summary
Review the discovery notes and identify the one or two dominant values the client expressed. Every recommendation will be connected back to these. If you cannot remember the client's values clearly, the plan is not ready to be built.
Step 2 — Map the gaps across the four Fundamentals
For each Fundamental, identify what exists and what is missing. Cash flow: what is the real surplus? Protection: which of the four risks are unaddressed? Growth: what is not aligned to the client's goals and timelines? Tax efficiency: where are the three stages unplanned? Each gap is a potential recommendation.
Step 3 — Prioritize by urgency and values alignment
Not every gap becomes a recommendation in the first plan. Prioritize by two criteria: urgency — what would cause the most harm if left unaddressed; and values alignment — what did the client say matters most. A protection gap for a client who values family security is both urgent and values-aligned. It goes first.
Step 4 — Size each recommendation to the surplus
Every recommendation has a cost — a premium, a contribution, a reallocation. The total of all recommendations cannot exceed what the surplus supports. If the complete set of recommendations exceeds the available surplus, prioritize ruthlessly. A plan the client cannot afford is not a plan.
4F Solution — Lesson 3: The one-page client summary

The client leaves with one page — not a binder

The one-page summary is not a compliance document. It is the client's plan in plain language — simple enough to explain to a partner, clear enough to act on, and direct enough that every item on it connects to something they said in the discovery meeting.

What belongs on the one-page summary
Five sections only: the client's stated values, their current financial picture across the four Fundamentals, the gaps identified, the recommendations with values connections, and the agreed next steps. If it does not fit on one page, the plan is too complicated to execute.
Example — one-page client summary
What matters most to you
Family security. Financial independence by 60. Leaving something behind for your children.
Your current picture
Cash Flow
$800 monthly surplus after expenses and debt
Protection
Group life only. No disability or critical illness coverage.
Growth
Savings account only. No registered accounts in use. No investment alignment to goals.
Tax Efficiency
No registered contribution room used. No retirement income plan. Estate unplanned.
What we are recommending — and why
1. Personal disability coverage — Because you told us family security matters most, and your income is the only thing standing between your family and financial exposure if you cannot work.
2. Term life insurance — Because you told us you want your family taken care of if something happens to you. Your current group coverage ends if you leave your employer.
3. Registered retirement account contributions — Because you told us you want financial independence by 60. Starting now with $400/month gives you the best chance of reaching that goal.
4. Tax-efficient growth structure review — Because you have contribution room you are not using and a savings account that is not working as hard as it could for your retirement goal.
Next steps
Applications for disability and life coverage. First registered contribution set up. Follow-up meeting in 90 days to review progress and address growth and tax structure.
Why one page matters
A thick report signals complexity. A single page signals clarity. The client who walks out with one page understands their plan. The client who walks out with a binder rarely does. The professional's job is not to demonstrate how much they know — it is to make the client feel clear, confident, and ready to act.
4F Solution — Lesson 4: The solution delivery meeting

The solution delivery meeting has a structure — follow it every time

The solution delivery meeting is not a pitch. It is a conversation that reconnects the client to what they told you, shows them where their current situation falls short, and presents a clear path forward — in that order.

1
Open by reflecting what you heard
"Before I show you what I put together, I want to make sure I captured what matters most to you. You told me that family security is your priority, that financial independence by 60 is the goal, and that you want to leave something behind for your children. Did I get that right?"
2
Show the current picture honestly
"Here is where you are across the four areas we looked at — cash flow, protection, growth, and tax efficiency. I want to be direct with you about what I found, because some of what I am going to show you is a gap between where you are and where you told me you want to be."
3
Present each recommendation with its values connection
For each recommendation: state what it is, connect it explicitly to what the client told you, and explain what remains at risk if it is not addressed. Never present a recommendation without its why.
4
Confirm the total cost against surplus
"Everything I am recommending today totals [amount] per month. Based on your cash flow picture, that is within what you have available. I want to make sure that feels right to you before we move forward."
5
Close with clear next steps
"Here is what happens next. [Specific actions, timelines, paperwork.] I will follow up with you in [timeframe] to make sure everything is in place and to look at what we deferred for later."
4F Solution — Lesson 5: Connecting recommendations to values

Every recommendation has a because — and the because is always a value

A recommendation without a values connection is a product pitch. A recommendation anchored to something the client told you is financial advice. The language that connects them is what makes the difference.

Protection recommendation
Value stated: "I want to make sure my family is taken care of."
Recommendation: Personal disability insurance
"You told me that your family's security is what matters most. Right now, if you became unable to work — not because of something that ended your life, but because of an illness or injury — your income stops and your family's lifestyle is immediately at risk. This coverage ensures that does not happen. It is the most direct way to protect the thing you told me matters most."
Growth recommendation
Value stated: "I want to be financially independent by 60."
Recommendation: Registered retirement account contributions — $400/month
"You told me that financial independence by 60 is the goal. Starting $400 a month in a registered account from today gives you the best realistic path to reaching that. Every month we delay, the compound growth that would have worked for you does not. This is the most important thing we can start today for your long-term goal."
Tax efficiency recommendation
Value stated: "I want to leave something behind for my children."
Recommendation: Permanent life insurance for estate transfer
"You told me you want to leave something meaningful behind for your children. Without a structure in place, the tax event at your estate could significantly reduce what they actually receive. A permanent policy ensures the full amount reaches them — outside the estate, tax-free, regardless of when that day comes."
The formula — use it every time
"You told me [value or goal]. Right now, [current gap or risk]. This recommendation [what it does] — which means [what it protects or enables for the client]." Every recommendation. Every time. No exceptions.
4F Solution — Lesson 6: Worked example

From discovery to plan — a complete example

This is how the full 4F process looks from start to finish with a real client scenario. Follow the thread from values through to recommendations.

The client — discovery summary
Sarah and David, both 38. Two children, ages 6 and 9. Combined household income — primarily David's, Sarah works part-time. Values expressed: family security, freedom from debt, leaving something behind for their children. Goals: pay off the mortgage in 12 years, fund the children's education, retire at 62.
Cash Flow
Monthly surplus of $950 after all expenses and debt payments. David's income is the primary source — Sarah's is variable. No emergency reserve currently in place.
Protection
David has group life insurance through his employer — 2x salary, not portable. No disability coverage. No critical illness. No personal life insurance. Sarah has no coverage of her own. Income loss reserve: zero.
Growth
Small savings account. No registered accounts in use. No education savings for either child. No investment aligned to the retirement or mortgage goals. Contribution room available and unused.
Tax Efficiency
No registered contribution room used. No income splitting strategy in place despite the income gap between David and Sarah. Estate unplanned — no permanent coverage, no will reviewed recently.
The plan — prioritized and values-connected
1. Emergency reserve ($200/month until 3 months built) — Because the plan cannot absorb income disruption with zero buffer. This protects everything else.

2. Personal disability coverage for David ($180/month) — Because family security is the stated priority and David's income is the engine. If it stops, nothing else in the plan survives.

3. Term life for David — portable, personal ($95/month) — Because the group coverage disappears if he leaves his employer and the mortgage, education, and retirement goals all depend on his income continuing or being replaced.

4. Education savings — registered accounts ($200/month split) — Because funding the children's education was named as a goal and time horizons for both children are 9 and 12 years respectively.

5. Registered retirement contributions ($275/month) — Because retiring at 62 requires starting now. This is sized to what remains of the surplus after the priorities above are funded.

Total: $950/month — exactly matched to available surplus.
4F Solution — Lesson 7: Handling objections

Objections are not rejections — they are requests for clarity

When a client pushes back on a recommendation, they are usually telling the professional one of three things: they do not understand it, they do not trust it, or they do not feel the urgency. The response to each is different.

"This is more than I expected to spend."
"I understand — let me show you how we got here. Every item on this plan connects to something you told me matters to you. If we need to phase it, we can — but I want to be honest with you about what we are leaving unaddressed if we do. Which of these feels least urgent to you right now, and let us look at what that means."
"I want to think about it."
"Of course — this is an important decision. Can I ask what specifically you want to think through? Sometimes there is a question I can answer right now that makes the decision clearer. And I want to make sure you know that a couple of these — particularly the protection items — are time-sensitive in a way that the others are not."
"I already have coverage through work."
"That is a good starting point — and I factored it in. What concerns me is that group coverage is tied to your employment. If you leave, are made redundant, or your employer changes the benefit, that coverage disappears. The recommendation I am making is personal coverage that stays with you regardless of what happens at work."
"Can we just do one thing for now?"
"Absolutely — if we are going to prioritize, let me show you which item on this plan addresses the most urgent risk relative to what you told me matters most. I would rather we do the most important thing properly than try to do everything partially. Here is where I would start."
The principle behind every response
Never abandon the values connection when handling an objection. Every response brings the client back to what they told you they care about. An objection handled from a values foundation is advice. An objection handled from a product foundation is selling.
4F Solution — Lesson 8: The ongoing relationship

The plan is a living document — and life will change it

The solution delivery meeting is not the end of the process. It is the beginning of a relationship that compounds in value every year — for the client and for the professional's practice.

The core principle
A financial plan built today reflects today's reality. Income changes. Families grow. Goals are achieved and replaced by new ones. Markets move. Tax rules shift. The professional who stays close to the client's life stays relevant — and irreplaceable. The one who disappears between transactions is always one conversation away from being replaced.
48 hours — the implementation window
Speed after the solution delivery meeting builds more confidence than anything said in the room. Within 48 hours: send a written summary of what was agreed, initiate all paperwork, and confirm the follow-up meeting date. A client who hears nothing after saying yes begins to question the decision. A client who receives a clear, prompt follow-up feels they made the right choice.
The 90-day review — the relationship deepens here
Book this before leaving the solution delivery meeting. The 90-day review is a structured conversation — not a check-in call. Confirm everything agreed upon is in place. Review cash flow for any changes since the first meeting. Surface items that were deferred from the first plan. And ask, genuinely: "Is there anyone in your life — a family member, a colleague, a friend — who you think would benefit from the kind of conversation we had?" A client who has been well served answers that question naturally.
When life happens — plan adaptability
Life does not wait for annual reviews. The professional who is positioned as a trusted resource — not just a product provider — gets the call when something significant changes. These are the moments that matter most:

Income change — a promotion, a job loss, a career shift, or a business milestone changes the cash flow picture and every Fundamental that depends on it.

Family change — a new child, a marriage, a separation, or the death of a parent changes protection needs, growth timelines, and tax structures simultaneously.

Health event — a diagnosis, a disability, or a recovery changes protection coverage, income, and in some cases the entire retirement picture.

Windfall or inheritance — a lump sum of any size triggers a complete 4F review. Surplus changes, growth opportunities expand, and tax efficiency becomes immediately relevant.

Goal achieved — a mortgage paid off, a child graduated, a business sold. Every completed goal frees surplus and opens the next chapter of the plan.

The professional who knows about these events — because the client tells them — is the one who adapts the plan before the gap appears, not after.
The annual review — the 4F conversation, again
Every year, run the 4F conversation as if it were the first meeting — because for at least one Fundamental, the picture will have changed. Ask about cash flow: has anything shifted? Ask about protection: is existing coverage still adequate? Ask about growth: are the timelines and vehicles still aligned to the goals? Ask about tax efficiency: is the structure still optimized? The client who receives a genuine annual review — not a portfolio report, but a full values-to-fundamentals conversation — does not need to look for another professional.
Building a practice — the value compounds too
A client well served at every stage of life refers. They refer because they understand their own plan — and because they have seen what happens when someone treats their financial life seriously. A practice built on the 4F framework does not grow through cold calling or product promotions. It grows through clients who can explain to a friend exactly what their professional does and why it is different from anyone they have worked with before. That conversation — the referral — is the most powerful marketing the profession has ever known. It cannot be manufactured. It can only be earned.
What the 4F Solution builds
A client who understands why every recommendation was made is a client who stays. A client whose plan adapts when their life changes is a client who deepens. A client who can explain their plan to someone they care about is a client who refers. The 4F Solution is not just a planning process — it is the foundation of a practice built on trust, clarity, and genuine advice at every stage of the client's life.
4F Solution — Lesson 9: Summary review

The complete 4F process — from first conversation to ongoing relationship

Before the knowledge check, consolidate everything covered across all seven modules.

The 4F Solution in one paragraph
The professional begins with a values conversation — surfacing what the client cares about most. They then discover the client's picture across four Fundamentals: Cash Flow, Protection, Growth, and Tax Efficiency. Each conversation educates while it discovers. No recommendations are made during discovery except where a critical and obvious gap requires immediate attention. The professional builds a complete plan from the discovery notes, connects every recommendation to a stated value or goal, and presents it at a dedicated follow-up meeting. The client leaves with a one-page summary they can understand and act on. The relationship continues through regular reviews that keep the plan aligned to the client's evolving life.
The rule that governs every recommendation
"I am recommending this because you told me that _____ matters to you, and without it, _____." If that sentence cannot be completed, the recommendation does not belong in the plan.
The ongoing relationship — five touchpoints
1
48-hour follow-up — Confirm what was agreed, initiate all paperwork, and book the 90-day review before the client leaves the room.
2
90-day review — Confirm implementation, check cash flow changes, surface deferred items, and invite referrals naturally.
3
Life events — Income change, family change, health event, windfall, goal achieved. Each one triggers a plan review — before the gap appears, not after.
4
Annual review — Run the full 4F conversation again. Every year, at least one Fundamental will have changed.
5
Practice growth — Clients who understand their plan refer. The referral is earned, not manufactured.
Objections — the principle
Every objection response brings the client back to their stated values. An objection handled from a values foundation is advice. An objection handled from a product foundation is selling.
What separates the 4F professional
Most professionals lead with products. The 4F professional leads with values — and every product recommendation that follows is the inevitable conclusion of a conversation the client recognizes as being about their own life. That is the difference between being sold to and being advised. It is also the difference between a client who stays and one who leaves.
